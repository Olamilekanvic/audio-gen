[instructive] The Federal Reserve has three main tools. Think of them as three different dials on the same control panel. Each one affects the economy differently. And the Fed uses all three — sometimes together, sometimes separately — depending on what the economy needs.
Tool one: The Federal Funds Rate — The Price of Money.
This is the most famous one. The one you hear about on the news every time the Fed meets.
The federal funds rate is the interest rate that banks charge each other for overnight loans. When banks lend money to each other at night — which happens constantly, to balance their books — they charge this rate.
Why does that matter to you? Because when this rate goes up, borrowing gets more expensive across the entire economy... Banks have to pay more to borrow from each other. They pass that cost on to you in the form of higher mortgage rates, higher car loan rates, higher credit card rates.
When the rate goes down, the opposite happens. Borrowing gets cheaper. Mortgages get more affordable. Businesses can borrow to expand and hire... Spending picks up. The economy warms up.
Think of the federal funds rate like the water pressure in a building. When the pressure is high — money flows freely through every pipe in the system. Every loan, every mortgage, every business credit line gets fed easily... When the Fed lowers pressure — money flows slower. Borrowing gets harder. Spending cools down.
The Fed raises the rate to fight inflation. It lowers the rate to fight recession. And it meets eight times a year — every six weeks or so — to decide whether to move it, hold it, or change it.
In 2022 and 2023, the Fed raised rates eleven times in eighteen months — the most aggressive rate hiking cycle in forty years... Each hike was felt immediately by anyone with a variable-rate mortgage, a credit card balance, or a business loan.
Tool two: Reserve Requirements — The Safety Buffer.
Here's something most people don't know about banks. They don't keep all of your money in the vault.
When you deposit $1,000 at Chase, Chase doesn't just sit on it. They lend most of it out to other customers as mortgages, car loans, and business credit... That's how banks make money — they pay you a small interest rate on your deposit and charge a higher rate to borrowers. The difference is their profit.
But this creates a risk. If too many customers show up at once demanding their money — like in the Panic of 1907 — and the bank has lent most of it out, they can't pay everyone. The bank fails.
To prevent this, the Fed sets reserve requirements — a minimum percentage of deposits that banks must keep on hand and cannot lend out.
Think of it like a restaurant's food safety rules. The health department doesn't run the restaurant. But it sets minimum standards — you must keep food above a certain temperature, wash hands regularly, maintain certain hygiene conditions... The restaurant operates within those standards. The rules protect the customers.
Reserve requirements protect bank customers the same way. They ensure there's always a minimum buffer of real cash available — so the bank can't lend out every single dollar and leave itself completely exposed.
In March 2020, the Fed actually lowered reserve requirements to zero to encourage banks to lend as much as possible during the COVID crisis... It was a dramatic move — and a reminder that these tools are not fixed. The Fed adjusts them based on what the economy needs in the moment.
Tool three: Open Market Operations — Buying and Selling.
This is the most technical-sounding tool — but the concept is actually simple once you see it clearly.
Open market operations means the Fed buys and sells government bonds — called US Treasuries — in the financial market. And by doing this, it directly controls how much money is flowing through the economy.
Here is how it works. When the Fed wants to put more money into the economy, it buys government bonds from banks... It pays for those bonds with money it creates — electronically, out of thin air. The banks receive cash. They now have more money to lend. More lending means more spending. The economy gets stimulated.
When the Fed wants to take money out of the economy — to cool things down, fight inflation — it sells government bonds back to banks... The banks pay for those bonds with cash. That cash goes to the Fed and effectively disappears from circulation. Less money in the system. Less lending. Less spending. The economy cools.
Think of the economy like a swimming pool. The water is money. Open market operations is the Fed controlling the taps... Buy bonds — open the tap, more water flows in, the pool fills up. Sell bonds — open the drain, water flows out, the pool empties. The Fed can flood the economy with cash or drain it — just by buying and selling bonds.
During COVID in 2020, the Fed bought trillions of dollars worth of bonds — flooding the pool. It was the fastest and largest expansion of the money supply in American history... It kept credit flowing and stopped the economy from seizing up entirely. But it also contributed — alongside other factors — to the inflation surge that followed in 2021 and 2022.
Every tool has consequences. The Fed knows this. And it uses these three levers constantly — adjusting, calibrating, responding — in real time to an economy that never sits still.
[seriously] Three tools. The federal funds rate controls the price of borrowing. Reserve requirements control the safety buffer. Open market operations control the actual flow of money. Together they give the Fed extraordinary power over every corner of your financial life.
